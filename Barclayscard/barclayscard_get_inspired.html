Joi Gordon, CEO, Dress for Success Worldwide

When I graduated from college, I began my career in the legal profession as an assistant district attorney in the Bronx, New York. The job was the means to an end to gain experience in litigation and, while the cases were interesting, I found myself wanting to be more inspired by my daily work.

During that time, I was introduced to Dress for Success just shortly after it opened its doors, a non-profit organization that at the time was popularly known for providing interview suits to low-income women. Soon after Dress for Success was founded, it came to recognize that finding a job is only one step in a woman's journey towards economic independence and that remaining employed and building a rewarding career is essential if a woman is to become self-sufficient.

I wanted to get involved, so I joined its Board of Directors. As I became more and more involved, I realized the experience held a great deal of purpose for me. I no longer wanted to be responsible for putting women behind bars; I wanted to be the one that helped get them ahead of the game, so that they could have prosperous futures, stable families and create deeper ties to their communities.

So I decided to leave the courtroom and build on my experience in the boardroom, becoming the executive director of Dress for Success’ New York program in 1999.

My change in careers was definitely a cause for concern for some, but I soon realized that other people’s perceptions didn’t have to be my own.

I think it’s important for women to find within themselves their own inner motivations. I’ve always believed whether it’s joining boards or getting involved with an organization in some other capacity—if you don’t try, you’ll never know what winning might feel like.

Nerdwallet Blog

Specifically as a woman, it’s hard to find time to deal with your money when you’re juggling work, family, friends and the all-too-often-forgotten personal time. It can be overwhelming to be paying bills, commuting, working and dealing with everything around the house. I’ve sometimes let my own finances slip, whether it’s missing a credit card payment when I was sick, forgetting to pay a bill when traveling abroad or catching a stolen card a little bit too late.

Whether you’re a big city dweller or reside in a smaller town, everyone’s finances are at risk. Between losing your credit card, using an unsecure network and even swiping your card at a major retailer, there are plenty of chances to have your identity stolen. Outside of just avoiding the worst case of identity theft, setting up a system for your finances can have far more benefits.

By exercising good money management skills, investing upfront time understanding finances and simply automating and double checking your payments, you will gain peace of mind, save precious time and gain control so you can easily avoid common mistakes that have a big impact.

The Dollar Stretcher Blog

We all like to dress well. You can spend a lot on your wardrobe. In fact the average family spends over $1700 annually on clothing.

But, it's not necessary. And, you don't have to shop the clearance racks or thrift shops if you don't want to (although for some the bargain hunting is the most enjoyable part of shopping). There are some simple tricks to have a stylish wardrobe for less.

Trendy clothes look old quickly and a bargain that you can only wear a few times before you discard it is not inexpensive. Even if you got it on sale. You can increase the flexibility of everything you buy if it goes with other previously purchased items. They'll seem newer longer if you can match them with more things in your closet. Plus, you won't bring something home and realize that you don't have anything to go with it.

MoneywiseMoms Blog

“It’s impossible. Don’t even try.”

That’s what my husband and I said when we looked at our debt in 2011 and realized what little progress we had been making each year as we paid the minimum payments. Something had to change, or that big $79,000 lump would always be there.

“We did it.”

That’s what we can say now in 2014. Now that debt lump is gone and instead there’s money filling an emergency fund. We made one big change--we took each paycheck and put a significant amount towards the debt first, then lived off the remainder. That commitment turned the tide for us by changing our outlook and really impacting the debt. We made lots of little changes, too--reducing our expenses in every budget category, earning extra money selling household items and babysitting, and putting off any unnecessary spending. Every dollar saved or earned went towards the debt.

Can you do it?

Yes, you can. You can release yourself from the hold that debt has on you. Each time a paycheck comes, it can be your money instead of being sent away towards debt. Every little (and big) effort reduces the debt. It will be difficult, but it will be worth it.

Helpful Tips

Clarify Your Path

Get clear on your vision, your mission and milestones required to achieve your goals. Give those milestones small actionable steps with a time stamp to make it happen. In simple terms: Write a business plan.

-Mom Biz Coach Blog

There is a very good chance you have monetized a skill or talent. You were not put on this planet to be everything to everyone. You were put on this planet to deliver great value with the support of a knowledgeable and powerful team. I know you think you can’t afford to outsource – you actually can’t afford not to.

-Mom Biz Coach Blog

Find an expert with a proven track record of helping others overcome the challenges you are facing. Look for social proof of their impact on others – testimonials.
If you don’t invest in yourself, no one else will.

Negotiate Your Salary

Here are some strategies to start with:

- Check how much people in your industry and in similar positions with equivalent knowledge and experience are making. If you want to negotiate from a position of strength, it is critical to know where you stand in relation to your peers.

- Learn how to talk about your achievements in relation to how they affect your company’s bottom line. Whenever possible use numbers to quantify your accomplishments.

- Anticipate questions that your boss will ask and work from a win-win perspective. What’s important to your manager? Why do they need you? What is the current demand for your position in the market? Women are particularly well attuned to the needs of others. Leverage this advantage!
